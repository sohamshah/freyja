§ 1What a covered call is, and what it isn't solid

A covered call is a long stock position with a short out-of-the-money call written against it. The writer collects an upfront premium and accepts a cap on upside in exchange. The trade has positive expected value when implied volatility exceeds realized volatility — a condition that has historically held for major equity indices, though the magnitude of the premium has shrunk in low-vol regimes.

§ 2The income-vs-upside tradeoff solid

For SPY, monthly 1-σ OTM calls have historically produced gross premium of roughly 0.4–0.6% per month, against a dividend yield of about 1.5% per year. Premium captures the volatility risk premium; dividends do not. The cap on upside has historically cost the BXM index 2–4 percentage points in the strongest deciles of monthly returns.

§ 3Assignment risk partial

Assignment is most likely just before ex-dividend dates and at in-the-money strikes; on assignment the writer delivers the underlying at the strike. This is straightforward in a tax-advantaged account.

§ 4Tax treatment partial

Premium received on expiry is treated as short-term capital gain. If the call is assigned, the resulting stock sale uses the strike price plus premium as the proceeds. The wash-sale interaction is non-trivial in a taxable account and is not yet covered here.
