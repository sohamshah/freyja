all mockups / goal mode
goal-based execution · design exploration

Fourteen ways to make convergence legible.

Goal mode is fundamentally different from tasks. The user gives the system one objective and a turn budget; the agent runs a try-judge-retry loop until a small judge model rules the goal complete or the budget runs out. The dashboard's job isn't to enumerate work — it's to show whether we're getting closer.

The first seven mocks (pass one) explored convergence assuming the judge produces a meaningful numeric — confidence percentages, distance to a target, a sparkline that climbs. That works for verifiable goals like "47 tests pass." It breaks down for the more common case where the judge's signal is qualitative — "have you researched this thoroughly enough?" The last three mocks (pass two) drop the number entirely and design around lists of considerations, open questions, and marked-up drafts.

pass one example · verifiable Refactor the authentication module to pass all 47 unit tests without breaking the login flow. Currently turn 8 of 20, confidence 0.74, judge says continue. There was a regression at turn 5 (a backoff attempt broke long-lived tokens). The login flow integration test now passes; three SessionTokenExpiry failures remain.
pass two example · qualitative Research covered call options thoroughly enough to give a grounded recommendation for a $250k retirement portfolio. Currently turn 5 of 15. The judge has settled five subtopics (mechanics, the income/upside tradeoff, capital-gains treatment, etc.) and is still asking five more (wash-sale interaction inside an IRA, strike-selection framework, alternatives, sequence-of-returns risk, recent regulation). There's no number to climb — the judge accepts the answer when it runs out of objections.
01
Convergence over enumeration.
There's only one thing being worked on. Don't make the user count tasks. Show direction, distance, and pace.
02
Two voices, one loop.
The judge is a distinct actor from the agent. Most variants give the judge its own visual treatment — soft violet, italic serif — so its reasoning reads as an outside opinion.
03
Show the trajectory, not the snapshot.
A regression at turn 5 is more important than the current 0.74. Most mocks treat the shape of the past 8 verdicts as the primary signal.
v1 · glance-first
Compass
open →
A calm watch-face. One large confidence ring, the goal above it, the latest verdict reason in italic serif below. Turn budget shown as a row of small dots. Designed for the case where you want to know "where are we?" in a single glance and nothing more.
Best when the user is checking in periodically and trusts the loop. Most peripheral-vision-friendly variant — could even live as a small floating window or menu-bar dropdown.
v2 · trajectory-first
Heartbeat
open →
Confidence as a sparkline plotted across the turn budget. Each judge verdict is a dot; the regression at turn 5 reads instantly as a dip. A "now" line marks the latest, dashed projection extends to the budget ceiling. Annotations call out inflections.
Best when trend is what matters — converging, plateauing, oscillating, or decaying. The most data-instrument-like variant, with the calm of a heart monitor rather than the noise of a stock chart.
v3 · narrative-first
Court
open →
Each turn is a "hearing" — the agent reports what it did, the judge issues a ruling in italic serif with a confidence delta. Reads like a court transcript. Older turns above, current turn highlighted at the bottom. Closest to language; furthest from charts.
Best when the value of goal mode is the agent's reasoning, not just the outcome. Most readable; most humane; calmly pairs with the v5 narrative variant from tasks mode.
v4 · document-first
Living Brief
open →
A working document the agent rewrites every turn. Sections: where we are, what we've learned, what we've tried, what's left, latest verdict. New text glows briefly when written. The user can click any section to edit and redirect the loop.
Best when the user wants to collaborate with the agent rather than just observe it. Most editable; most likely to make goal mode feel like a writing tool. Naturally produces a postmortem when the goal completes.
v5 · spatial-first
Approach
open →
The goal as a literal bullseye. Each attempt is plotted around the target — closer to center as confidence rises, further out on regressions. A faint trail connects the eight attempts so far; the current position glows. The dotted "done" ring at 0.85 is the threshold to pass through.
Best when the user wants visceral feedback. Makes oscillation and approach legible at a glance. Most distinctive aesthetic — best as a "feature view" rather than the always-on default.
v6 · transparency-first
Two Voices
open →
A split screen — agent on the left, judge on the right, with a center column of turn markers tying them together. Each agent action sits horizontally aligned with the judge ruling that followed it. Lets the user address either party from the cradle: judge: relax the threshold.
Best when trust in the judge is uncertain. Makes the agent–judge interplay fully transparent and lets the user nudge either one without ending the loop. The most "lift the hood" variant.
v7 · attention-first
Reflection
open →
Inverts the always-on dashboard. The system runs quietly in the background and only summons your attention at deliberate pause points — a centered card with what the agent learned, what it'd try next, where it's stuck (if anywhere), and a five-button row to continue / redirect / extend / change / stop. Auto-resumes if you don't intervene.
Best when you don't want to babysit the loop. Most respectful of the user's attention; closest to Raskin's principle that the interface should offer information rather than demand it. Could ship as a setting layered on top of any other variant.
pass two · qualitative goals
For goals where the judge's signal is prose, not a percentage. No fake numbers, no thresholds to cross — just considerations to address until there are no more objections.
v8 · breadth-first
Coverage Map
open →
A constellation of subtopics the judge thinks need to be addressed. Solid green nodes are settled, accent-tinted nodes are partially covered, dashed outlines are not yet touched. New nodes appear in violet when the judge realizes "actually, you also need to think about…" A side panel reads what's still missing in plain prose.
Best when the goal has a discoverable surface area — the agent doesn't know upfront what counts as "thorough" and the judge expands the territory as the work proceeds. Makes the unknown unknowns visible.
v9 · ledger-first
Open Questions
open →
A two-column register: settled (left, green) vs still asking (right, violet). Each open question is the judge's specific concern, in italic prose, with a priority tag (must / should / may). Items migrate left as the agent answers them; new ones can appear on the right when the agent's work raises follow-ups. The number of open questions is the progress signal.
Best when the goal is research-shaped. Most directly answers "what would the judge say is missing right now" — and the user can drop questions they consider out of scope ("drop the 1256 question") right from the cradle.
v10 · artifact-first
Draft + Critique
open →
The agent assembles an actual draft answer; the judge marks it up like an editor with anchored comments — looks good, needs evidence, blind spot, required. Each draft section has a status badge (solid / partial / missing); placeholder boxes flag what's not yet written. The draft is the deliverable; "done" means no more outstanding objections.
Best when the goal produces a written artifact the user will actually read — a recommendation, a memo, a research summary. Naturally produces a postmortem because the final draft is the result. Most concrete; closest to how a human editor and writer would converge on a piece.
pass three · the iteration as form
Visualizations that take the back-and-forth itself as the subject. Drawn from natural forms — woven fabric, winding spirals — to make the iterative loop legible at a glance. Both work for either numeric or qualitative goals.
v11 · weave-first
Tapestry
open →
Each turn weaves a row of fabric. Vertical warp threads in agent-accent run top to bottom; a horizontal weft thread in judge-violet undulates over and under across them. Tight regular rows = strong agreement; the regression at turn 5 shows up as a broken yellow row with a snag. The current row is being woven now — the shuttle is mid-flight, weft halfway across, judge's reply pending.
Best when the texture of the back-and-forth matters as much as the destination. The full iteration history is encoded in a single textile sample — you can read it like tree rings. Most novel of the iteration-form variants.
v12 · approach-first
Spiral
open →
Turns wind inward toward the goal. One continuous curve, eight arcs so far, with the regression visible as a small outward kink before the spiral closes back in. Future turns shown as a faint dashed continuation. The goal glows softly at the center, drawing the spiral toward it. Like a nautilus shell or a fingerprint.
Best when you want a single image that captures the whole iterative journey at once. The most cinematic variant — a quietly powerful demo for showing what goal mode actually does. Pairs well with v2 Heartbeat as data + form.
pass four · briefing the judge
A control surface for the judge that treats it as a colleague rather than a hidden component. The deepest move available in goal mode — most everything else is reading the loop; this lets you actually shape it.
v13 · meta-control
Judge Brief
open →
The judge's configuration written as a memo TO the judge, not a settings page. Six editable sections: criteria (with a three-tier must / should / may taxonomy plus a provisional tier the judge surfaces but you must approve), voice (prose, not toggles), what evidence the judge can see, when it may add new criteria, what it must never do, and termination conditions. A live preview pane shows the verdict the judge would issue under the current brief, plus counterfactuals: "if you remove this criterion, this becomes done."
Best when the judge is the bottleneck. Every other variant assumes the judge is well-tuned; this one is how you get there. The cradle accepts direct address — judge: be stricter on the strike framework — and the judge replies with proposed edits to its own brief. Closest to how a working editor and writer actually negotiate scope.
pass five · the recommendation
A synthesis variant that combines the three strongest moves for qualitative goals — Open Questions (v9), Draft + Critique (v10), and Judge Brief (v13) — into one workspace. Built strictly on data shapes that fall out of any qualitative goal session: a structured judge response, a draft the agent maintains, and anchored comments. Nothing bespoke.
v14 · recommended for qualitative goals
Qualitative Studio
open →
Three columns, one workspace. Left: the open-questions ledger (asking + settled), with priority pills and the just-added provisional question highlighted. Center: the draft as an actual evolving document, with status badges per section and gutter-dot indicators where the judge has anchored a comment. Right: a tabbed rail that swaps between Marginalia (the eight anchored judge comments — looks-good, needs-evidence, blind-spot, required) and Brief (compact editable criteria + voice + live preview with counterfactuals). Hover a draft paragraph to highlight its comment and vice versa.
Use this as the default for qualitative goals. Every element here corresponds to data the agent and judge already produce on every turn — structured criteria with status, a markdown draft, and section-anchored comments. No bespoke visualizations, no per-goal tuning. The brief stays one click away in the right rail rather than a separate page, so the user can tighten a criterion mid-loop without losing sight of the draft. Ship hint at top is gated on must coverage.
