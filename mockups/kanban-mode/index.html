Five ways to make multi-agent dispatch calmer.

Today's kanban dashboard packs eight metric cards, a board-pulse carousel, an agent roster, an autopilot strip, a dispatch ticker, a watch list, a judge verdict panel, and a message bus into a single screen. It tries to show everything at once — and as a result fails to clarify the three things that actually matter in kanban: dispatches (who got what, when), progress (how the board is moving), and assignments (which agent is on which card).

Each mock below picks a different position on what to keep and what to delete. The first three are direct displays; the fourth is a control surface; the fifth is the recommended synthesis.

working example used throughout Migrate the analytics service from Postgres to Snowflake. 14 cards, four agents (scout, forge, sentinel, archivist), autopilot on. 4 done · 3 in flight · 5 ready · 1 blocked on legal · 1 stale (scout claimed an audit 24m ago and went quiet). The dispatcher wants to send Run shadow reads against Snowflake next, prefers scout (preferred for research), but scout is stale — so the fallback is archivist after dual-write lands.
01
People before columns.
Kanban's distinguishing feature isn't its columns — it's that multiple agents are in motion at once. Dashboards organized around who's doing what are calmer than dashboards organized around which column things are in.
02
Make the dispatcher visible.
Autopilot quietly makes the most consequential decisions in the loop. Its next move, and its reasoning, deserve the same surface area as a human operator's would.
03
One number, not eight.
A single header line — 4 done · 3 in flight · 1 stale · next dispatch in 15s — replaces the metric carpet. Counts return inline only where they're load-bearing.
v1 · board-first
The Plate
open →
The simplest possible kanban: four columns (ready · in flight · blocked · done), each card carrying a small agent chip + duration. In-flight cards have a soft accent tint and a spinner; the stale card surfaces a warn-red badge in the in-flight column. Click any card to open the viewer — a slide-in drawer with the full ticket: assignment, brief, dependencies (✓ done upstream + ○ queued downstream), chronological activity timeline (tool calls, edits, claims, autopilot decisions), files touched, and operator actions.
Best when the user already knows kanban deeply and wants the most familiar possible view. Most direct mapping from the data model. Closest to Linear's current sprint view in spirit.
v2 · agents-first
The Crew
open →
Each agent gets a station: name, role, current card front-and-center, recent activity below. Stale claims surface as a red-tinted station with a "reclaim & ask what happened" button. A right rail carries the small queue + blocked list + dispatch log. The dashboard now answers "who's doing what?" as its first question — kanban's actual differentiator. Click any station or queue card to open the viewer — same six-section drawer as v1.
Best when the user thinks of the loop as a small team at work rather than a board with cards. Most humane interpretation; surfaces stalls and idle agents naturally.
v3 · narrative-first
The Standup
open →
Reads like a daily standup writeup. One paragraph per agent ("forge is writing the column-level migration scripts — fourteen minutes in…"). The stale agent gets called out in italics: "worth reclaiming and asking what happened." Blocked work and the queue live in their own short paragraphs at the bottom, with autopilot's next move highlighted as an inline aside.
Best when the user steps in periodically rather than continuously monitoring. Most readable. The variant most likely to make the loop feel like a colleague rather than a process.
v4 · meta-control
Dispatcher's Desk
open →
The autopilot's configuration written as a memo TO the dispatcher (parallel to the Judge Brief in goal mode). Four sections: who can claim what (a 4×5 yes/prefer matrix), policies (stale threshold, retry policy, circuit breaker, max in flight, spend cap — each as a sentence with the value as a chip), when to ask the operator (three radios, default "on stalls, regressions, and risky cards"), and what you should never do. A live preview shows the next dispatch decision under the current rules and three counterfactuals.
Best when the autopilot is misbehaving or the loop is producing awkward dispatches. Every other variant assumes the dispatcher is well-tuned; this one is how you get there. Direct address from the cradle: dispatcher: prefer sentinel for verify · loosen stale to 15m.
pass two · the recommendation
A synthesis that combines the strongest moves from above — agents-first focal point (v2), narrative dispatcher reasoning (v3), and one-click access to the dispatcher's brief (v4) — without losing the at-a-glance board.
v5 · recommended for kanban
The Bridge
open →
Three columns, one workspace. Left: the crew — each agent's station with current card, last finished, and (for the stale claim) a reclaim button. A "dispatcher's next move" footer card explains what autopilot will do in 15 seconds, in plain prose. Middle: the board itself — ready / blocked / done, each ready card showing its preferred agent as a colored chip; the next-up card carries an amber "next" tag matching the dispatcher's intent. Right: the live dispatch feed, with autopilot events in italic dispatcher-color so they're distinguishable from agent claims and completions. A small dispatcher card at the bottom of the feed restates the next decision and links straight to the editable brief (v4). Clicking any station or board card opens the same six-section viewer; the cradle shifts left so it doesn't overlap.
Use this as the default for kanban. The left column answers who's doing what, the middle answers what's queued and where we are, the right answers what just happened. The dispatcher's intent is visible in three places at once (the crew footer, the next-tag on the queue card, the bottom-of-feed card) without being repetitive — each surfacing serves a different glance. The brief is one click away rather than a separate page.
